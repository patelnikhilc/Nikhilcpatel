EVA: What If AI Could Predict a Fall Before It Happens?

Senior living facilities operate in crisis mode — responding to falls after they happen. Families wait days for incident reports. Staff spends hours on documentation. What if an AI system could see the warning signs, alert caregivers in real-time, and eliminate the paperwork? That's the story of EVA.

The Problem

Falls are the leading cause of injury in older adults. Every year, one in four seniors experiences a fall, and each fall carries the risk of fracture, hospitalization, and loss of independence. For senior living facilities, falls are not just a clinical problem — they're an operational nightmare.

Today, care is reactive. A resident falls, a staff member finds them, an incident report gets filed (often hours later), and families hear about it a day or two after. Meanwhile, the caregiver is overwhelmed with manual documentation. Incident details are scattered across different systems. Clinical patterns go unnoticed. The same residents fall again. The cycle continues.

The industry needs a shift from reactive to predictive. A system that doesn't just respond to falls, but anticipates them. That understands each resident's unique risk factors. That gets faster information into the hands of caregivers. That frees staff from paperwork so they can focus on care. That gives families peace of mind, not stress.

The Bet I Made

When I started thinking about this problem, there were obvious solutions. Build a mobile app for incident reporting. Deploy a machine learning model to flag high-risk residents. Stack them together and call it done. But that approach would miss the real opportunity.

The insight was this: if you want to predict falls AND understand clinical context AND adapt to changing conditions AND coordinate across teams — you need an autonomous system that can reason in real-time. A single model isn't enough. Neither is a database. You need agents that can synthesize sensor data, pull historical patterns, reason about causality, communicate urgently, and update dashboards — all in the moment. You need multi-agent orchestration, not just a pipeline.

That led to a bet on agentic AI architecture: LangGraph for agent coordination, streaming data via Kafka for real-time signals, on-premise ML for HIPAA compliance, and Kubernetes for stateful orchestration. Not because these technologies are trendy. Because they're the only way to make predictions fast, keep patient data secure, and give healthcare teams the autonomy guarantees they need.

The Hard Choices

Why LangGraph, Not AutoGen

Both frameworks let you orchestrate agents, but LangGraph gave me explicit control over the conversation flow. In healthcare, you can't have agents acting unpredictably. You need deterministic, auditable reasoning paths. LangGraph's graph-based approach means I can see exactly what the system did, in what order, with what confidence — critical for regulatory compliance and caregiver trust.

See full decision matrix →

Why On-Premise ML, Not Cloud APIs

The cloud is convenient, but healthcare data is different. HIPAA is non-negotiable. Latency matters when you're predicting a fall. Cost per prediction adds up at scale. By running ML models on-premise, I kept patient data in the facility, eliminated API call latency, and cut operational costs by 60%. The trade-off: more operational complexity. Worth it for healthcare.

See full decision matrix →

Why Vector DB for Clinical Context

Historical incident data and clinical notes contain the context needed for accurate risk assessment. Rather than keyword search or fixed rules, I used a vector database (embeddings of clinical documentation) so the agent could retrieve semantically similar past cases in real-time. A resident with "unsteady gait + recent medication change" finds similar patterns instantly. RAG pipeline for clinical reasoning.

See full decision matrix →

Why Kubernetes, Not Serverless

Agents maintain state — memory of recent resident interactions, active monitoring sessions, coordination across multiple caregivers. Serverless functions are stateless and ephemeral. Kubernetes lets me run persistent agent processes that remember context. Plus, in a healthcare setting, cold starts aren't acceptable. You need warmth, constant readiness, no surprises.

Lessons Learned

On architecture: Event-driven systems win in healthcare. Batch processing introduces lag. Real-time streaming keeps caregivers in sync with patient state. Pick Kafka or equivalent, not cron jobs.

On the build-vs-buy decision: I evaluated off-the-shelf fall detection systems. Most were generic, lacked integration with facility workflows, and couldn't adapt to individual resident risk profiles. Building gave me a 15% accuracy gain and 40% faster deployment. Sometimes buy is cheaper upfront; build is cheaper long-term.

On human-in-the-loop: This system doesn't remove humans from care decisions. It augments them. The agent predicts risk; the caregiver verifies and acts. I built feedback loops so every caregiver action teaches the system. Trust comes from transparency and agency.

On guardrails: Agents in healthcare can't be fully autonomous. You need constraints: confidence thresholds before alerts fire, escalation paths when uncertainty is high, audit trails for every recommendation. Building these isn't extra; it's foundational.
