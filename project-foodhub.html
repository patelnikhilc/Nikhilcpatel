Turning 1,898 Orders Into a Revenue Growth Playbook

A NYC food delivery platform was leaving money on the table. Weekend demand was 2.5x weekday volume, weekday deliveries were 26% slower, and 39% of customers never left a rating. I dug into the data and built a strategic playbook with 6 actionable recommendations.

The Question

FoodHub is a food aggregator connecting NYC customers with hundreds of restaurants through a single app. Revenue comes from a tiered commission model: 25% on orders above $20, 15% on orders above $5. The business was growing but leadership needed data-driven answers to critical questions: Where is demand concentrated? Why are deliveries slower on certain days? Which cuisines drive revenue vs. ratings? And where are the untapped growth pockets?

I set out to answer 17 specific business questions using exploratory data analysis, then translate the findings into a strategic playbook that leadership could act on immediately.

What the Data Revealed

55.6%

Two Cuisines Dominate Revenue

American and Japanese cuisines account for more than half of all orders and $3,439 in combined commission. Shake Shack alone drives 11.5% of total volume (219 orders). But dominance creates fragility — the platform's revenue is concentrated in two cuisine categories.

2.5x

Weekend Demand Dwarfs Weekday

71.2% of all orders happen on Saturday and Sunday. Weekday volume is anemic by comparison. This isn't just a demand pattern — it's an operations problem. Weekend surge means driver shortages, longer prep queues, and capacity planning headaches. But it also means weekday is an untapped growth channel.

26% Slower

Weekday Deliveries Are Broken

Average weekday delivery takes 28.3 minutes vs. 22.5 minutes on weekends — a 26% gap. Counter-intuitive: you'd expect weekends to be slower due to higher volume. The data suggests weekday driver allocation is insufficient. Fewer orders doesn't mean faster — it means fewer drivers on the road.

4.5-4.8★

Hidden Gems in the Long Tail

Spanish, Thai, and Indian cuisines have the highest average ratings (4.5-4.8 stars) despite low order volume. These aren't underperforming cuisines — they're under-promoted cuisines. Customers who find them love them. The opportunity: targeted marketing to shift volume toward high-satisfaction categories.

Revenue Deep Dive

The commission model creates a clear revenue lever: orders above $20 generate 25% commission vs. 15% for smaller orders. Only 29.2% of orders cross the $20 threshold — but they contribute 60% of total commission revenue.

The math is straightforward. Moving just 5% of orders from the $15-20 range above $20 (through bundling, upsells, or minimum-order promotions) would increase commission revenue by an estimated 12-15% with zero new customer acquisition.

Meanwhile, the top 3 customers (by order frequency) represent disproportionate lifetime value. A 20% loyalty discount for these power users would cost pennies compared to the retention value.

The Playbook: 6 Strategic Recommendations

Each recommendation is tied directly to a data finding. No gut feelings, no hand-waving — every action item traces back to the numbers.

01

Scale Weekend Operations

Increase delivery capacity on Saturday and Sunday to capture unmet demand. 71.2% of orders concentrated in 2 days means driver allocation must match.

02

Fix Weekday Delivery Speed

Audit driver routing, add weekday incentives, coordinate restaurant pickup windows. 26% slower delivery on lower-volume days signals a resource allocation gap.

03

Close the Ratings Gap

38.8% of orders go unrated — a blind spot for quality monitoring. Implement post-delivery push notifications with loyalty point incentives for rating completion.

04

Grow High-Satisfaction Cuisines

Spanish, Thai, and Indian restaurants score 4.5-4.8 stars but have low volume. Targeted in-app campaigns could shift 5-10% of orders toward these categories.

05

Accelerate High-Value Orders

Promote premium bundles and minimum-order incentives to push more orders above the $20 threshold. Moving 5% of mid-range orders up yields 12-15% revenue lift.

06

Activate Loyalty Programs

Top 3 customers by frequency represent outsized LTV. 20% vouchers for power users plus featured restaurant placements for high-rated partners creates a flywheel.

Lessons Learned

On data storytelling: The most important analysis isn't the most complex. The weekend-vs-weekday delivery speed finding is a simple group-by comparison, but it unlocks a multi-million dollar operational insight. Complexity for its own sake is noise.

On revenue modeling: Understanding the commission tier structure before running EDA changed every question I asked. The 25%/$20 threshold made "average order value" the single most actionable metric in the dataset. Always understand the business model before touching the data.

On the ratings gap: 38.8% unrated orders isn't a data quality issue — it's a product design issue. The delivery app's rating flow is optional and buried. A nudge at the right moment (post-delivery, while the food is being eaten) would close this gap. The data pointed to a UX problem, not a data problem.

On communicating to leadership: I built an executive presentation alongside the notebook because the audience for analysis isn't other analysts — it's decision-makers with 5 minutes. Six numbered recommendations, each tied to a specific data point, is the format that gets budgets approved.
